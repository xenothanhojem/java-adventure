# How to Use This Study Guide

This guide is designed to help you prepare for a Grade 10 IEB Business Studies test where you may need to write a **business report** using knowledge from **forms of ownership**, **professionalism**, and **ethics**.

You should not study only definitions. You must be able to:

1. Explain concepts in full sentences.
2. Compare different forms of ownership.
3. Apply theory to a business scenario.
4. Recommend a suitable form of ownership for a business.
5. Identify ethical and unethical business behaviour.
6. Explain why professionalism matters in business.
7. Write a structured business report with headings, findings, conclusions, and recommendations.

---

# Test Skill Focus: Business Report Writing

## What a Business Report Is

A **business report** is a formal written document that presents information, analysis, conclusions, and recommendations about a business issue or problem.

In Business Studies, a report usually asks you to:

- Investigate a business problem or situation.
- Explain relevant business theory.
- Apply that theory to the case study or scenario.
- Make justified recommendations.

A report is not written like an essay. It must be structured, clear, factual, and professional.

---

# Section 1: Business Report Format

## 1.1 Standard Business Report Structure

A strong Grade 10 business report should usually include the following sections:

```text
Title
Terms of Reference / Introduction
Procedure / Methodology
Findings
Conclusion
Recommendations
```

Depending on your school’s format, the wording may differ slightly, but the key idea remains the same: the report must be organised and easy to follow.

---

## 1.2 Title

The title tells the reader exactly what the report is about.

### Example

**Report on the Most Suitable Form of Ownership for Green Harvest Farms**

### What to remember

A title should be:

- Short.
- Specific.
- Formal.
- Linked directly to the business scenario.

---

## 1.3 Terms of Reference / Introduction

The introduction explains why the report is being written.

It should answer:

- Who requested the report?
- What is the report investigating?
- What issue or decision is being considered?

### Example

This report has been prepared to investigate the most suitable form of ownership for Green Harvest Farms as it expands from a small farming business into a larger agricultural company. The report considers the advantages and disadvantages of sole traders, partnerships, private companies, and public companies.

---

## 1.4 Procedure / Methodology

This section explains how the information was gathered or what sources were used.

### Example

Information was gathered from the Grade 10 Business Studies textbook, class notes, ownership comparison tables, and the given business scenario.

### What to remember

Do not make this section too long. It is usually worth only a few marks, but it shows that the report is formal.

---

## 1.5 Findings

The findings are the main body of the report.

This is where most of your marks will come from.

In the findings section, you should:

- Explain the relevant concepts.
- Compare options.
- Discuss advantages and disadvantages.
- Link your answer to the scenario.
- Use business terminology.

### Example headings for findings

```text
5.1 Sole Trader
5.2 Partnership
5.3 Private Company
5.4 Public Company
5.5 Professionalism and Ethics
```

---

## 1.6 Conclusion

The conclusion sums up the main findings.

It should not introduce new information.

### Example

In conclusion, Green Harvest Farms requires more capital, better continuity, and limited liability as it expands. A sole trader or partnership would not be suitable in the long term because these forms of ownership have limited capital and unlimited liability. A private company would provide a stronger legal structure while still allowing the owners to control who may own shares.

---

## 1.7 Recommendations

Recommendations explain what the business should do next.

A recommendation must be:

- Practical.
- Linked to the findings.
- Justified with reasons.

### Example

It is recommended that Green Harvest Farms registers as a private company because this will give the owners limited liability, allow the business to raise more capital privately, improve business continuity, and create a more professional image. The owners should also draft a Memorandum of Incorporation and appoint directors to manage the company effectively.

# Section 3: Key Business Concepts and Definitions

You must know these definitions well. Many marks are lost when learners vaguely explain concepts instead of using correct business language.

## 3.1 Business Concept

A **business concept** is the basic idea of a business, including what product or service it offers, who its customers are, and how it plans to make money.

### Example

A mobile coffee cart that sells coffee to office workers before work.

---

## 3.2 Business

A **business** is an organisation that produces goods or services to satisfy needs and wants while aiming to make a profit.

### Example

A shop selling groceries to customers.

---

## 3.3 Capital

**Capital** is the money, equipment, buildings, vehicles, tools, or other resources needed to start and operate a business.

### Example

A farmer uses capital to buy land, seeds, equipment, and irrigation systems.

---

## 3.4 Debt

**Debt** is money that a business or person owes to another party, usually because money has been borrowed.

### Example

A business loan from a bank that must be repaid with interest.

---

## 3.5 Liability

**Liability** refers to legal responsibility for the debts or obligations of a business.

### Example

If a business cannot repay its debts, someone may be legally responsible for paying them.

---

## 3.6 Unlimited Liability

**Unlimited liability** means that the owner or partners are personally responsible for the debts of the business. If the business cannot pay its debts, the owners may lose personal assets such as a house, car, or savings.

### Example

A sole trader’s business fails, and the owner must sell personal property to repay suppliers.

---

## 3.7 Limited Liability

**Limited liability** means that owners or shareholders only risk the amount they invested in the business. Their personal assets are usually protected.

### Example

A shareholder in a private company loses only the money invested in shares if the company fails.

---

## 3.8 Legal Personality / Legal Persona

A business has a **separate legal personality** when the law sees the business as separate from its owners. The business can own property, enter contracts, sue, and be sued in its own name.

### Example

A private company can sign a lease agreement in the company’s name.

---

## 3.9 Continuity

**Continuity** means the ability of a business to continue operating even when ownership changes, an owner dies, or shareholders change.

### Example

A public company continues operating even when shareholders sell their shares.

---

## 3.10 Shareholder

A **shareholder** is a person or organisation that owns shares in a company.

### Example

A person who owns shares in MTN is a shareholder.

---

## 3.11 Dividend

A **dividend** is a portion of company profits paid to shareholders.

### Example

Shoprite may pay dividends to shareholders after making a profit.

---

## 3.12 Memorandum of Incorporation — MOI

The **Memorandum of Incorporation**, or **MOI**, is a legal document that sets out the rules, rights, duties, and responsibilities of a company and its shareholders.

### Example

A private company’s MOI may explain voting rights and share rules.

---

## 3.13 CIPC

The **Companies and Intellectual Property Commission**, or **CIPC**, is the South African body responsible for registering companies and intellectual property.

### Example

A private company must register with the CIPC.

---

## 3.14 Tax

**Tax** is money paid to the government to fund public services.

### Example

Businesses pay tax to SARS.

---

## 3.15 Corporate Tax

**Corporate tax** is tax paid by registered companies on their profits.

### Example

A private company pays corporate tax to SARS.

---

## 3.16 Income Tax

**Income tax** is tax paid by individuals on income earned.

### Example

Employees pay income tax through PAYE deductions.

---

# Section 4: Forms of Ownership — Overview

A **form of ownership** refers to the legal structure of a business. It determines:

- Who owns the business.
- Who controls the business.
- Who is responsible for debts.
- How profits are shared.
- How much capital can be raised.
- How the business is taxed.
- Whether the business continues if ownership changes.

---

## 4.1 Main Forms of Ownership to Know

For this test, focus on:

1. Sole trader / sole proprietor.
2. Partnership.
3. Private company — Pty Ltd.
4. Public company — Ltd.
5. State-owned enterprise — SOE.
6. Non-profit organisation — NPO / NPC.

You must be able to explain, compare, and apply each of these to business scenarios.

---

# Section 5: Sole Trader / Sole Proprietor

## 5.1 Definition

A **sole trader** is a business owned and controlled by one person.

The owner provides the capital, makes the decisions, receives the profits, and is responsible for the debts of the business.

### Example

A barber who owns and operates his own barber shop.

---

## 5.2 Characteristics of a Sole Trader

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| One owner | The business is owned and controlled by one person. | A food vendor owns and manages the stall alone. |
| Unlimited liability | The owner is personally responsible for all business debts. | If the business fails, the owner may have to sell personal property. |
| No separate legal personality | The owner and the business are legally the same. | Contracts are signed in the owner’s name. |
| Easy to establish | Few legal requirements are needed to start. | A learner starts a car-washing business. |
| Owner receives all profits | The owner keeps all profits after expenses. | A tuckshop owner keeps the profit from sales. |
| Limited continuity | The business may close if the owner dies, retires, or becomes unable to work. | A small family business closes when the owner passes away. |
| Direct relationship with customers | The owner deals directly with customers. | A hairstylist builds personal relationships with clients. |
| Limited capital | The business depends mainly on the owner’s savings and loans. | A sole trader struggles to buy new equipment. |

---

## 5.3 Advantages of a Sole Trader

### 5.3.1 Easy and inexpensive to start

A sole trader is simple to establish because there are few legal formalities. The owner does not need to register a company with the CIPC.

### 5.3.2 Full control over decisions

The owner can make quick decisions without consulting partners, directors, or shareholders.

### 5.3.3 Owner keeps all profits

The owner does not have to share profits with partners or shareholders.

### 5.3.4 Personal customer service

Because the owner is directly involved, the business can build strong relationships with customers.

### 5.3.5 Simple taxation

The owner pays personal income tax on business profits rather than company tax.

### 5.3.6 Privacy

A sole trader does not have to publish financial statements publicly.

### 5.3.7 Easy to close down

The owner can close the business without complicated company procedures.

---

## 5.4 Disadvantages of a Sole Trader

### 5.4.1 Unlimited liability

This is one of the biggest disadvantages. The owner is personally responsible for business debts.

### 5.4.2 Limited capital

The business may struggle to raise enough money for expansion because it relies mostly on the owner’s savings, profits, or personal loans.

### 5.4.3 Lack of continuity

The business may end if the owner dies, retires, becomes ill, or sells the business.

### 5.4.4 Heavy workload and pressure

The owner often performs many roles, such as manager, marketer, accountant, buyer, and customer service provider.

### 5.4.5 Limited skills and expertise

One person may not have all the skills needed to manage every part of the business.

### 5.4.6 Difficult to expand

Growth is often limited because capital and management expertise are limited.

### 5.4.7 Long working hours

Sole traders may need to work long hours because they manage most tasks themselves.

### 5.4.8 Difficult to attract investors

Investors may be reluctant to invest because the business does not have a separate legal personality or shares.

---

## 5.5 When Is a Sole Trader Suitable?

A sole trader is suitable when:

- The business is small.
- The owner wants full control.
- Start-up costs are low.
- The business does not require large capital.
- The owner has the skills to manage the business.
- The risk of debt is relatively low.

### Suitable examples

- Hairdresser.
- Tuckshop.
- Food stall.
- Tutor.
- Photographer.
- Small repair business.
- Car wash.

---

# Section 6: Partnership

## 6.1 Definition

A **partnership** is a business owned by two or more people who agree to share profits, losses, responsibilities, skills, and capital.

---

## 6.2 Characteristics of a Partnership

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Minimum of two owners | A partnership must have at least two partners. | Two attorneys open a legal practice. |
| Partnership agreement | Partners usually create an agreement explaining profit sharing, duties, capital contributions, and decision-making. | Partners agree that profits will be divided equally. |
| Unlimited liability | Partners may be personally responsible for the debts of the business. | A partner may lose personal savings if the partnership cannot repay debts. |
| Shared decision-making | Major decisions are usually discussed and agreed on by partners. | Partners decide together whether to open another branch. |
| No separate legal personality | The partnership and partners are legally regarded as the same. | Contracts are signed in the partners’ names. |
| Shared profits and losses | Profits and losses are shared according to the partnership agreement. | One partner receives 60% and another receives 40%. |
| Limited continuity | The partnership may dissolve if a partner dies, retires, or leaves. | A medical partnership closes when one doctor retires. |
| Combined skills and resources | Each partner contributes different skills, knowledge, capital, or experience. | One partner handles finance while another handles marketing. |

---

## 6.3 Advantages of a Partnership

### 6.3.1 More capital available

Partners combine their financial resources, which allows the business to access more capital than a sole trader.

### 6.3.2 Shared workload

Responsibilities can be divided among partners, reducing the pressure on one person.

### 6.3.3 Greater skills and expertise

Each partner may bring different skills. For example, one partner may be good at finance while another is good at marketing.

### 6.3.4 Better decision-making

Partners can discuss ideas and solve problems together.

### 6.3.5 Relatively easy to establish

Partnerships have fewer legal requirements than companies.

### 6.3.6 Wider network

Partners may bring different contacts, customer relationships, and professional networks.

### 6.3.7 Shared risks

Losses and risks are shared among partners instead of being carried by one person alone.

---

## 6.4 Disadvantages of a Partnership

### 6.4.1 Unlimited liability

Partners may be personally liable for business debts.

### 6.4.2 Conflict between partners

Disagreements about money, roles, workload, or business decisions can damage the partnership.

### 6.4.3 Shared profits

Profits must be divided among partners, so each partner receives less than if they owned the business alone.

### 6.4.4 Lack of continuity

The partnership may end if a partner dies, leaves, or retires.

### 6.4.5 Slower decision-making

Important decisions often require consultation and agreement.

### 6.4.6 One partner’s actions affect all partners

If one partner acts irresponsibly, the other partners may suffer financial or reputational damage.

### 6.4.7 Difficult to sell or transfer ownership

It can be difficult for a partner to leave or sell their share without agreement from the other partners.

### 6.4.8 Limited growth opportunities

Partnerships may still struggle to raise large amounts of capital compared with companies.

---

## 6.5 Partnership Agreement

A **partnership agreement** is a written agreement between partners that explains how the partnership will operate.

Although a partnership agreement can be verbal, it is strongly recommended that it is written to prevent misunderstandings and disputes.

---

## 6.6 What Must Be Included in a Partnership Agreement?

| Item | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Name of the partnership | The official name under which the business operates. | Mokoena & Dlamini Attorneys |
| Names and details of partners | Full names, addresses, ID numbers, and contact details. | Information about each owner is recorded. |
| Nature of the business | Description of what the business does. | Operating a legal practice or farming business. |
| Capital contributions | The amount of money, assets, or resources each partner contributes. | One partner contributes R100 000 and another contributes machinery. |
| Profit-sharing ratio | How profits and losses will be divided. | Profits shared equally or 60:40. |
| Roles and responsibilities | Duties of each partner. | One partner manages finance, another manages marketing. |
| Decision-making procedures | How decisions will be made. | Major decisions require agreement from all partners. |
| Salaries or drawings | Whether partners may withdraw money from the business. | Monthly drawings are allowed up to a set amount. |
| Banking arrangements | Who may sign cheques or authorise payments. | Both partners must approve large payments. |
| Admission of new partners | Conditions for adding new partners. | New partner requires approval from existing partners. |
| Withdrawal or retirement | What happens if a partner leaves. | Remaining partners may buy the leaving partner’s share. |
| Dissolution of partnership | How the partnership will be closed. | Assets are sold and profits distributed. |
| Dispute resolution | How disagreements will be settled. | Mediation before legal action. |
| Working hours and commitment | How much time each partner must contribute. | Each partner works full-time in the business. |
| Confidentiality clause | Prevents partners from sharing confidential information. | Partners may not share customer lists with competitors. |

---

## 6.7 Why a Partnership Agreement Is Important

A partnership agreement is important because it:

- Prevents misunderstandings.
- Reduces conflict.
- Protects partners legally.
- Clarifies profit sharing.
- Explains responsibilities.
- Provides procedures for disagreements.
- Helps with business continuity if a partner leaves or dies.
- Improves accountability.

---

## 6.8 When Is a Partnership Suitable?

A partnership is suitable when:

- The business needs more capital than one person can provide.
- The owners want to combine skills.
- The business is professional or service-based.
- The partners trust one another.
- The business does not yet need to become a company.

### Suitable examples

- Attorneys.
- Doctors.
- Accountants.
- Consultants.
- Farming partnerships.
- Small construction businesses.
- Family grocery stores.

---

# Section 7: Private Company — Pty Ltd

## 7.1 Definition

A **private company**, or **Pty Ltd**, is a registered company that is privately owned. It has a separate legal personality, limited liability, and shares that are not offered to the general public.

---

## 7.2 Characteristics of a Private Company

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Separate legal personality | The company exists separately from its owners. | A construction company signs contracts independently. |
| Limited liability | Shareholders are only responsible for the amount they invested. | Investors lose only their investment if the company fails. |
| Shares not sold to the public | Shares are privately owned and transferred only with approval. | Family members own shares in a business. |
| Continuity of existence | The company continues even if shareholders change, retire, or die. | A business continues after the founder retires. |
| Managed by directors | Directors manage operations and strategy. | A board manages a retail business. |
| Registered with CIPC | The company must register with the Companies and Intellectual Property Commission. | XYZ Traders Pty Ltd is registered with CIPC. |
| MOI | The Memorandum of Incorporation outlines rights, duties, and rules. | Rules for voting rights are included. |
| Ability to raise capital | The company may attract investors more easily than a sole trader or partnership. | Investors fund a technology start-up. |

---

## 7.3 Advantages of a Private Company

### 7.3.1 Limited liability

Shareholders’ personal assets are protected from business debts.

### 7.3.2 Easier access to finance

Banks and investors are often more willing to fund registered companies.

### 7.3.3 Business continuity

The company continues regardless of changes in ownership.

### 7.3.4 Professional image

A registered company is often viewed as more credible and professional.

### 7.3.5 Easier transfer of ownership

Shares may be transferred between shareholders, subject to company rules.

### 7.3.6 Separate management and ownership

Skilled managers and directors can run the business even if the owners are not involved daily.

### 7.3.7 Growth opportunities

Private companies are suitable for medium and larger businesses that want to expand.

### 7.3.8 Better long-term stability

The company structure supports sustainable growth.

---

## 7.4 Disadvantages of a Private Company

### 7.4.1 Expensive to establish

Registration and legal compliance costs may be high.

### 7.4.2 More legal requirements

The company must comply with the Companies Act and submit required documents.

### 7.4.3 Financial records required

Accurate accounting and reporting systems are compulsory.

### 7.4.4 Slower decision-making

Directors and shareholders may need meetings before decisions are made.

### 7.4.5 Double taxation risk

The company pays tax on profits, and shareholders may also pay tax on dividends.

### 7.4.6 Shares cannot be sold publicly

Private companies cannot raise money from the general public by selling shares on the JSE.

### 7.4.7 Administrative burden

Compliance paperwork and legal processes may be time-consuming.

### 7.4.8 Less privacy

Financial information may need to be shared with regulators and shareholders.

---

## 7.5 When Is a Private Company Suitable?

A private company is suitable when:

- The business is growing.
- Owners need limited liability.
- The business needs more capital.
- The owners want a professional image.
- The business wants continuity.
- The owners still want control over who owns shares.

### Suitable examples

- App development companies.
- Construction companies.
- Food manufacturing businesses.
- Engineering companies.
- Fintech businesses.
- Larger farms.
- Retail chains.

---

# Section 8: Public Company — Ltd

## 8.1 Definition

A **public company**, or **Ltd**, is a company that may sell shares to the public and may be listed on a stock exchange such as the Johannesburg Stock Exchange — JSE.

---

## 8.2 Characteristics of a Public Company

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Shares sold to the public | Shares can be bought by members of the public. | MTN shares are traded publicly. |
| Separate legal personality | The company exists independently from shareholders. | Shoprite enters contracts in its own name. |
| Limited liability | Shareholders are not personally responsible for company debts. | Investors lose only their investments. |
| Managed by board of directors | Directors manage operations and strategic decisions. | Standard Bank’s board manages banking operations. |
| Large capital base | Public companies can raise large amounts of money from investors. | Sasol funds major projects. |
| Continuity | The company continues even when shareholders change. | Anglo American continues despite investor changes. |
| Strict legal requirements | Public companies must follow JSE and Companies Act requirements. | Annual reports and audits are compulsory. |
| Public accountability | Shareholders and the public expect transparency and good governance. | Publishing annual financial statements. |

---

## 8.3 Advantages of a Public Company

### 8.3.1 Raises large amounts of capital

Selling shares to the public provides significant funding for expansion.

### 8.3.2 Limited liability

Shareholders’ personal assets are protected.

### 8.3.3 Business continuity

Ownership changes do not affect company existence.

### 8.3.4 Professional management

Skilled directors and executives manage operations.

### 8.3.5 Increased credibility

Public companies are often trusted by investors, customers, and suppliers.

### 8.3.6 Expansion opportunities

Large capital supports national and international growth.

### 8.3.7 Shareholders can receive dividends

Investors may benefit financially from company profits.

### 8.3.8 Shares are transferable

Investors can buy and sell shares more easily.

---

## 8.4 Disadvantages of a Public Company

### 8.4.1 Expensive to establish

Listing on the JSE and complying with legal requirements can be expensive.

### 8.4.2 Strict legal requirements

Public companies must follow regulations, reporting rules, and governance standards.

### 8.4.3 Financial information becomes public

Competitors and the public can access financial information.

### 8.4.4 Slow decision-making

Large companies often need board approval and formal processes.

### 8.4.5 High administrative costs

Audits, shareholder communication, and compliance increase expenses.

### 8.4.6 Pressure from shareholders

Investors expect strong profits and dividends.

### 8.4.7 Risk of hostile takeovers

Other investors may attempt to gain control of the company.

### 8.4.8 Double taxation risk

Company profits and dividends may both be taxed.

---

## 8.5 When Is a Public Company Suitable?

A public company is suitable when:

- The business is very large.
- It needs significant capital.
- It plans national or international expansion.
- It can comply with strict legal requirements.
- It is ready for public accountability.

### Suitable examples

- Banks.
- Mining companies.
- Telecommunications companies.
- Insurance companies.
- Large food producers.
- Energy companies.

# Section 9: State-Owned Enterprise — SOE

## 9.1 Definition

A **state-owned enterprise**, or **SOE**, is a business that is fully or partly owned and controlled by the government.

SOEs often provide important services to citizens and businesses.

---

## 9.2 Characteristics of SOEs

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Government ownership | SOEs are fully or partly owned by government. | Eskom is owned by the South African government. |
| Provide essential services | SOEs provide services needed by citizens and businesses. | Transnet manages rail and port transport. |
| Separate legal personality | SOEs operate as separate legal entities. | PRASA can enter contracts in its own name. |
| Public accountability | SOEs are accountable to government and the public. | SOEs must report to Parliament and departments. |
| Focus on service delivery | Many SOEs focus more on public service than profit. | SABC provides educational and public-interest broadcasting. |
| Large-scale operations | SOEs often operate nationally and employ many people. | Eskom supplies electricity across South Africa. |

---

## 9.3 Advantages of SOEs

### 9.3.1 Provide essential services

SOEs ensure that citizens have access to important services such as electricity, transport, and communication.

### 9.3.2 Create employment

SOEs employ many South Africans.

### 9.3.3 Promote economic development

SOEs support infrastructure and national economic growth.

### 9.3.4 Government support

SOEs often receive financial support from government during difficult times.

### 9.3.5 Improve infrastructure

SOEs invest in roads, transport, energy, communication, and other infrastructure.

### 9.3.6 Services available to all citizens

SOEs may provide services even in areas where profits are low.

---

## 9.4 Disadvantages of SOEs

### 9.4.1 Political interference

Government involvement may affect business decisions.

### 9.4.2 Corruption and mismanagement

Poor management or corruption may reduce efficiency.

### 9.4.3 Financial losses

Many SOEs struggle financially and may need government funding.

### 9.4.4 Poor service delivery

Inefficiency may lead to unreliable services.

### 9.4.5 Dependence on taxpayers

Government may use taxpayers’ money to support failing SOEs.

### 9.4.6 Slow decision-making

Large structures and government processes may delay decisions.

---

## 9.5 Examples of SOEs in South Africa

| Industry | Example | Explanation |
|---|---|---|
| Electricity | Eskom | Generates and supplies electricity. |
| Transport | Transnet | Manages railways, ports, and pipelines. |
| Aviation | South African Airways | Provides airline services. |
| Finance | Land Bank | Provides loans to farmers and agricultural businesses. |
| Passenger rail | PRASA | Operates passenger rail services. |
| Broadcasting | SABC | Provides television and radio broadcasting. |
| Defence | Denel | Produces defence and military equipment. |

# Section 10: Non-Profit Organisation — NPO / NPC

## 10.1 Definition

A **non-profit organisation**, or **NPO**, is an organisation created to serve the community rather than to make profits for owners.

A **non-profit company**, or **NPC**, is a company form used for non-profit purposes.

---

## 10.2 Characteristics of NPOs

| Characteristic | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Public benefit objective | NPOs exist to help communities and solve social problems. | CANSA supports cancer awareness. |
| No profit distribution | Profits are reinvested into the organisation. | Donations are used to fund projects. |
| Depend on donations and grants | Many NPOs rely on fundraising, sponsorships, and donor support. | Gift of the Givers receives donations. |
| Community-focused | NPOs aim to improve society and support vulnerable groups. | SPCA protects animals. |
| Separate legal personality | Many NPOs register as legal entities. | WWF South Africa signs contracts in its own name. |
| Volunteer involvement | Volunteers often help with NPO activities. | Volunteers distribute food parcels. |

---

## 10.3 Advantages of NPOs

### 10.3.1 Support communities

NPOs help vulnerable and disadvantaged groups.

### 10.3.2 Address social problems

NPOs help reduce poverty, hunger, inequality, animal abuse, and environmental damage.

### 10.3.3 Tax benefits

Registered NPOs may receive tax exemptions or benefits.

### 10.3.4 Encourage social responsibility

NPOs promote ethical behaviour and community development.

### 10.3.5 Create employment and volunteering opportunities

NPOs may employ workers and provide skills development opportunities.

### 10.3.6 Improve quality of life

NPOs may provide healthcare, education, food, counselling, or support services.

---

## 10.4 Disadvantages of NPOs

### 10.4.1 Dependence on donations

NPOs may struggle if donations decrease.

### 10.4.2 Limited funding

Some NPOs lack enough money to expand services.

### 10.4.3 Unstable income

Funding can change from year to year.

### 10.4.4 Difficult to sustain operations

NPOs may close if funding stops completely.

### 10.4.5 High demand for services

NPOs often face more community needs than they can meet.

### 10.4.6 Limited resources and staff

# Section 11: Comparison of Forms of Ownership

## 11.1 Comparison Table

| Criteria | Sole Trader | Partnership | Private Company | Public Company | SOE | NPO / NPC |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Number of owners | One | Two or more | One or more shareholders | Many shareholders | Government owns fully or partly | Members, trustees, or directors depending on structure |
| Legal personality | No separate legal personality | No separate legal personality | Separate legal personality | Separate legal personality | Separate legal personality | May have separate legal personality if registered |
| Liability | Unlimited | Usually unlimited | Limited | Limited | Usually limited to entity | Usually limited if registered as NPC |
| Capital | Limited | More than sole trader but still limited | Can raise private capital | Can raise large capital from public | Government funded / supported | Donations, grants, sponsorships |
| Management | Owner | Partners | Directors | Board of directors | Government-appointed management | Board, trustees, or managers |
| Continuity | Limited | Limited | Good | Strong | Strong, but depends on government support | Depends on funding and governance |
| Profit distribution | Owner keeps profits | Partners share profits | Shareholders may receive dividends | Shareholders may receive dividends | Public service focus; may reinvest | Profits not distributed |
| Legal requirements | Few | Few, but agreement recommended | More legal requirements | Strict requirements | Government reporting | Registration and compliance if formalised |
| Best suited for | Small businesses | Professional services and small growing businesses | Medium to large private businesses | Large national or international businesses | Essential public services | Community and social causes |

---

## 11.2 Key Comparison Points to Memorise

### Sole trader vs partnership

A partnership has more owners, more capital, and more skills than a sole trader, but both usually have unlimited liability and limited continuity.

### Partnership vs private company

A private company has separate legal personality and limited liability, while a partnership usually does not.

### Private company vs public company

A private company cannot sell shares to the public, while a public company can. A public company can raise more capital but faces stricter legal requirements.

### Public company vs SOE

A public company is owned by shareholders and aims to make profit. An SOE is owned by government and often focuses on essential public services.

### NPO vs company

A normal company aims to make profit for owners or shareholders. An NPO exists to serve the community and does not distribute profits to owners.

# Section 17: Contracts and Contractual Obligations

## 17.1 What Is a Contract?

A **contract** is a legally binding agreement between two or more parties.

A contract creates rights and obligations for the parties involved.

---

## 17.2 Parties to a Contract

A contract normally involves:

- A person making an offer.
- A person accepting the offer.

### Example

A customer agrees to buy a cellphone contract from a service provider. The provider offers the contract, and the customer accepts the terms.

---

## 17.3 Requirements of a Valid Contract

For a contract to be valid, it should meet the following requirements:

| Requirement | Explanation |
|---|---|
| Contractual capacity | Parties must be legally able to enter into a contract. |
| Consensus | Parties must agree to the same terms. |
| Legal purpose | The contract must not involve illegal activities. |
| Possibility of performance | It must be possible to carry out the contract. |
| Formalities | If the law requires the contract to be in writing, that requirement must be followed. |
| Reasonableness and clarity | Terms should be clear and understandable. |

---

## 17.4 Breach of Contract

A **breach of contract** occurs when one party fails to fulfil the terms of the agreement.

### Examples

- A customer does not pay instalments.
- A supplier fails to deliver goods.
- An employee breaks confidentiality clauses.
- A tenant does not pay rent.
- A business provides goods that do not meet the agreed standard.

---

## 17.5 Why Contracts Are Important in Business

Contracts are important because they:

- Protect the parties legally.
- Clarify rights and responsibilities.
- Prevent misunderstandings.
- Provide proof of agreement.
- Explain what happens if one party fails to perform.
- Create trust between parties.

# Section 18: Types of Contracts

## 18.1 Employment Contract

An **employment contract** is an agreement between an employer and an employee.

It explains the employee’s job and the employer’s obligations.

### Common information in an employment contract

- Names and addresses of both parties.
- Job title.
- Job description and responsibilities.
- Remuneration structure.
- Leave details.
- Notice period.
- Code of conduct.
- Working hours.
- Confidentiality clauses.

---

## 18.2 Insurance Contract

An **insurance contract** is an agreement between the insurer and the insured.

The insured pays premiums, and the insurer agrees to cover certain risks under specific conditions.

### Example

A business insures its delivery vehicle. If the vehicle is damaged in an accident, the insurer may pay for repairs depending on the policy terms.

---

## 18.3 Lease Agreement

A **lease agreement** is a contract between a lessor and a lessee.

The lessor owns the goods or property, and the lessee uses it for a period in exchange for payment.

### Example

A business leases office space and pays monthly rent.

---

## 18.4 Instalment Sale Agreement

An **instalment sale agreement** allows a buyer to pay for goods over a period of time.

The buyer usually pays a deposit and monthly instalments. Ownership may pass to the buyer only after the full amount has been paid.

### Example

A business buys a delivery vehicle and pays it off monthly.

---

## 18.5 Rental Agreement

A **rental agreement** is a contract between a landlord and a tenant.

The tenant pays rent to use property for a certain period.

### Example

A tenant rents a shop space in a shopping centre.

---

## 18.6 Franchise Agreement

A **franchise agreement** allows one party, the franchisee, to operate a business using the name, brand, systems, and products of another business, the franchisor.

### Example

A person operates a fast-food outlet using the franchisor’s brand and business model.

- Partnership advantages: more capital, shared skills, shared workload.
- Partnership disadvantages: unlimited liability, conflict, shared profits, limited continuity.
- Private company advantages: limited liability, separate legal personality, continuity, easier access to finance, professional image.
- Recommendation: private company because the business wants to expand, attract investors, reduce personal risk, and improve credibility.

## Memo: Activity 10 — Business Report Guidance

A strong report should identify both professional and ethical issues.

### Problems

- Poor communication.
- Poor time management.
- Weak delegation or management.
- Unauthorised use of company computers.
- Misleading advertising.
- Weak organisational culture.

### Effects

- Lower productivity.
- Poor reputation.
- Customer complaints.
- Lower trust.
- Employee frustration.
- Possible legal or disciplinary consequences.

### Recommendations

- Create a code of conduct.
- Train employees on ethics and professionalism.
- Improve meeting discipline and time management.
- Set clear rules for company technology use.
- Correct misleading advertising.
- Improve communication systems.
- Encourage ethical leadership.
- Monitor behaviour and apply fair discipline.

# Section 34: Common Mistakes to Avoid

## Mistake 1: Writing an essay instead of a report

A report needs headings and structure.

## Mistake 2: Giving definitions only

You must apply theory to the scenario.

## Mistake 3: Saying “company” for every business

A sole trader and partnership are not companies.

## Mistake 4: Confusing private and public companies

Private company shares are not sold to the public. Public company shares may be sold to the public.

## Mistake 5: Forgetting liability

Liability is one of the most important comparison points.

## Mistake 6: Forgetting continuity

Continuity often determines whether a business can survive ownership changes.

## Mistake 7: Weak recommendations

A recommendation must be justified. Do not simply say “I recommend a private company” without explaining why.

## Mistake 8: Ignoring professionalism and ethics

If the scenario mentions dishonesty, advertising, employee behaviour, communication, contracts, or responsibility, link it to professionalism and ethics.

# Section 35: One-Page Quick Revision Summary

## Forms of ownership

| Form | Best for | Main advantage | Main disadvantage |
|---|---|---|---|
| Sole trader | Small business | Easy to start and full control | Unlimited liability and limited capital |
| Partnership | Two or more skilled owners | More skills and shared workload | Conflict and unlimited liability |
| Private company | Growing business | Limited liability and continuity | More legal requirements |
| Public company | Very large business | Raises large capital | Strict legal requirements and shareholder pressure |
| SOE | Essential public services | Public benefit and government support | Political interference and inefficiency |
| NPO / NPC | Community service | Social benefit | Dependence on donations |

Professional businesses should:

- Act honestly.
- Communicate clearly.
- Respect contracts.
- Manage time well.
- Avoid misleading customers.
